System prompt design

The assistant is scoped tightly to one job: diagnosing pricing and business-model problems from structured intake data, then handing back a fixed report shape (Executive Snapshot → Diagnosis → Issues Found → Recommendations → Next Step). Locking the section headers in the system prompt keeps the output parseable and consistent regardless of what the owner types in.

Two guardrails matter most here: grounding and honesty about gaps. The prompt explicitly forbids inventing competitor data or market stats that weren't provided, and instructs the model to name missing numbers rather than paper over them — a pricing tool that quietly guesses at your COGS is worse than useless. It also has a sparse-input fallback: if the form is mostly empty, the assistant says so and asks for the two or three data points it actually needs, instead of hallucinating a full report from a product description alone.

Scope is enforced with a redirect rule for off-topic requests, and there's a standing disclaimer that this isn't legal, tax, or accounting advice — appropriate for a strategy tool that touches real pricing decisions.

UI decisions

The visual language borrows from a bookkeeper's ledger rather than a generic dashboard: a sage-paper background with faint ruled lines, a red margin rule down the intake form (like columnar accounting paper), brass and wax-red accents for emphasis, and a serif/mono type pairing (Fraunces for headers, IBM Plex Mono for labels and figures) that reads like a financial document rather than a SaaS product.

The signature moment is the rubber-stamp element that lands on the report once the diagnostic completes — a small, decisive flourish that matches the "verdict" nature of the output without turning into a gimmick. Loading state uses a shimmering ledger-line skeleton instead of a spinner, so the wait still feels like "the numbers are being tallied" rather than a generic load.

The form itself mirrors what a real pricing consultant would ask in an intake call — industry, stage, price, cost, competitor pricing, pain point, goals — so filling it in doubles as a useful exercise even before the report comes back.

Future extensions

  • Tools: a live web-search tool to pull current competitor pricing automatically instead of relying on the owner's manual entry, and a margin-calculator tool the model can call to check its own arithmetic before stating a recommended price.
  • Memory: store each diagnostic per business so returning owners can compare this quarter's report to last quarter's, and the assistant can flag whether previous recommendations were acted on.
  • Multi-step workflows: turn this into a short back-and-forth — the assistant asks one clarifying follow-up before finalizing the report, or offers to draft the actual customer-facing pricing page copy once a recommendation is accepted. A/B test design (two price points, a hypothesis, and a success metric) would be a natural next output.
